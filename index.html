C-Days 2026 · CNCS · Alfândega do Porto · 16–18 Jun 2026

The AI Hype and the Silence of the Fundamentals

Everyone is talking about AI — agents that find zero-days on their own, models that write exploits, defensive tools with ever-bigger promises, and budgets reshaped to follow the conversation. But anyone working in incident response sees something different. Organizations keep falling for the same reasons they always have: someone gives up a password on a phone call, someone clicks a link that looks like it came from the CEO, a forgotten account with stale privileges sits exposed, a flat network lets one compromised endpoint see everything, MFA is deployed but riddled with exceptions. None of it is new, sophisticated, or in need of AI to be exploited. Kevin Mitnick wrote about this over twenty years ago. The techniques in The Art of Deception — impersonating authority, manufacturing urgency, exploiting good faith — still work exactly as they did in 2002. What changed is the cost: cloning a voice is cheaper than a coffee, tailoring a thousand phishing emails with real context takes minutes, and a deepfake on a video call is no longer fiction. AI didn't invent social engineering — it industrialized it. Yet we keep treating the problem as technical, buying another platform and adding another layer, while identities stay poorly managed, segmentation lives in the diagram but not the network, and awareness is a fifteen-minute annual module nobody takes seriously. This talk isn't about AI; it's about what AI is exploiting, and what we still fail to do despite knowing for decades that this is exactly what breaks. Before investing in the next defensive AI platform, it's worth asking how many real failures would be mitigated if we simply did well the things we've known how to do for twenty years. The weakest link is still human, the most common failures are still basic — and as long as we keep running from that to talk about AI, we'll keep losing for the same reasons we always have.

C-Days 2024 · CNCS · Coimbra

Collateral Damage & Lessons Learned from Cyberwarfare

When conflict goes digital, the blast radius doesn't stop at the border. Drawing on the war in Ukraine — where, as ENISA's Juhan Lepassaar put it, "attacks were often tried out in Ukraine before being expanded to EU countries" — this talk looks at the collateral damage cyberwarfare inflicts on organisations far from the front line, and the lessons defenders across Europe should already be applying.

C-Days 2022

C-Days 2022 · CNCS · Estoril

The Art of Mind Deception: A Practical Social Engineering exercise – phishing

From an information security standpoint, as human being, we are affected by social engineering tactics every day as an entry point to carry out technical hacking techniques. Easily deceived with psychological manipulation to perform actions or divulging information without our knowing. This situation leads us to reflect the importance of the growing evolution of this type of attacks that exploits this weakness. Despite the fact that organizations invest millions in next-generation security solutions, such as firewalls, endpoint protection, sandboxing, email protection, etc., there is a blind spot that has to be covered in the radar to spot the signs, the human being as a weakest link. There is no 'silver bullet' that can prevent cyberattacks at all, but it’s definitely necessary to boost the user awareness by making them more vigilant with the necessary knowledge to understand the daily risks in this complex cyberspace. In this paper, we describe our findings about the reasons why we are so vulnerable, the tools used by the attackers, a real phishing exercise and understand how can we mitigate this growing threat.

# About
